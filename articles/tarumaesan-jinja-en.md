## The Allure of This Sacred Site

### History and Origins

Tarumaesan Jinja's founding dates to 1875 (Meiji 8), during the early years of Hokkaido's colonization. The settlers developing the Tomakomai region established this shrine to seek protection from the active volcano Mount Tarumae and its potential eruptions.

The principal deity is Oyamatsumi no Kami, the great god of mountains, accompanied by Kukunochi no Kami (god of trees) and Kayanohime no Kami (goddess of fields). Oyamatsumi no Kami is the same deity enshrined at Oyamazumi Shrine in Ehime, the head shrine of mountain worship throughout Japan.

Through the Meiji and Showa periods, the shrine underwent several relocations and reconstructions, settling at its current location in 1936. The present buildings, rebuilt in 1983, feature robust construction designed to withstand Hokkaido's harsh climate.

### Architecture and Natural Features

The shrine buildings follow the shinmei-zukuri style foundation while incorporating distinctively steep roof angles‚Äîa regional adaptation to heavy snowfall. The main hall, worship hall, and shrine gate are all constructed from Japanese cypress, welcoming visitors with the pure fragrance of fresh wood.

The grounds encompass approximately 100,000 square meters (about 25 acres) surrounded by virgin forests of Ezo spruce and Sakhalin fir. Walking along the approach, visitors hear wild birds singing and may even encounter Ezo squirrels darting among the trees.

Most remarkable are the views from within the grounds. On clear days, visitors can see Lake Shikotsu, Mount Tarumae, and even the distant Pacific Ocean. The sight of Mount Tarumae illuminated by early morning light is particularly divine, attracting photography enthusiasts from far and wide.

### What Makes This Spot Special

What makes Tarumaesan Jinja exceptional is its veneration of an active volcano as its sacred body. Mount Tarumae is designated a Rank A active volcano by Japan's Meteorological Agency, maintaining ongoing volcanic activity. This energy from a "living mountain" is said to permeate the entire shrine.

As a shrine that has watched over the pioneering history of Hokkaido, Tarumaesan Jinja is believed to possess power that supports "new beginnings" and "overcoming difficulties"‚Äîembodying the pioneer spirit. ## Visitor's Guide

### Basic Worship Etiquette

1. **Bow at the torii gate**: Compose your mind before entering the sacred grounds
2. **Walk along the sides**: The center path is reserved for the deities
3. **Purify at the temizuya**: Rinse left hand, right hand, mouth, then left hand again
4. **Two bows, two claps, one bow**: At the worship hall, bow deeply twice, clap twice, then bow once
5. **Exit quietly**: Bow again when passing through the torii gate

### Special Points of Interest

**Main Hall and Worship Hall**: Magnificent shrine buildings from 1983. The fragrance of cypress pervades the solemn atmosphere.

**Mount Tarumae Viewing Platform**: A spot within the grounds offering direct views of Mount Tarumae. "Distant worship" facing the mountain is popular here.

**Sacred Tree**: An Ezo spruce estimated at 300 years old. Many visitors touch the trunk to receive its power.

**Inari Shrine**: An auxiliary shrine within the grounds, popular among those praying for business prosperity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How long does worship take?**
A: About 30 minutes for worship only; 60-90 minutes including grounds exploration. Lines for goshuin can be long during busy seasons, so allow extra time.

**Q: Is the shrine open in winter?**
A: Yes, it's open year-round. However, be sure to wear non-slip footwear as paths can be slippery with snow and ice.

**Q: Are pets allowed?**
A: Pets are not permitted within the shrine grounds.

**Q: Should I climb Mount Tarumae before visiting the shrine?**
A: If time permits, climbing Mount Tarumae is recommended, but the shrine alone offers sufficient sacred energy. For those concerned about physical ability, worshipping from the distant worship platform within the grounds is said to be equally effective.
