## The Allure of This Sacred Site

### History and Origins

Hokkaido Jingu's predecessor was the "Hokkaido Chinza Shinsai," established in Tokyo in 1869. At that time, the Meiji government was promoting Hokkaido's development as a national project, necessitating a shrine to enshrine guardian deities for the colonization effort.

In 1871, the shrine was relocated to Sapporo. Its permanent establishment at the current Maruyama location came in 1889. Originally called "Sapporo Shrine," it was renamed "Hokkaido Jingu" in 1964 when Emperor Meiji was enshrined as an additional deity.

The main deities are Okunitama no Kami, Onamuchi no Kami, Sukunahikona no Kami, and Emperor Meiji. The first three, known as the "Three Deities of Development," are gods governing land development and prosperity, and have watched over Hokkaido's growth since the beginning.

### Architecture and Natural Features

The main sanctuary follows the "shinmei-zukuri" style‚Äîthe same architectural form used at Ise Grand Shrine. The cypress bark roof and pristine white buildings create a pure beauty that stands brilliantly against Hokkaido's snowy landscape. The snow-draped shrine on a winter morning is breathtakingly divine.

The grounds span approximately 60 hectares of preserved primeval forest. Centuries-old Japanese elms and Mongolian oaks tower overhead while Ezo squirrels, Ezo red foxes, and about 80 species of wild birds inhabit this natural paradise.

In spring, approximately 1,400 cherry and plum trees burst into bloom, making this Hokkaido's premier flower-viewing destination. Seeing cherry blossoms and plum blossoms simultaneously is a spectacle unique to Hokkaido's climate.

### What Makes This Spot Special

Hokkaido Jingu's most distinctive feature is its abundance of energy for "pioneering" and "new beginnings." The prayers and determination of Meiji-era pioneers who challenged untamed wilderness still dwell within this shrine.

The orientation of the torii gates holds special meaning as well. The second torii faces northeast (the demon's gate direction), symbolizing prayers for Hokkaido's continued development and national defense awareness toward Russia.

The shrine is also famous as a "matchmaking" power spot. The Kaitaku Shrine within the grounds attracts many young couples seeking romantic fulfillment. The "love fortune" (koi-mikuji) is popular as a Hokkaido souvenir.

## Visitor's Guide

### Basic Worship Etiquette

1. **Enter from the main approach**: Bow at the first torii gate and calm your mind before entering.
2. **Purify at the temizuya**: Rinse left hand, right hand, mouth, left hand again, then handle.
3. **Walk along the sides**: The center path is reserved for the deities.
4. **At the Main Sanctuary**: Offer coins, bow twice, clap twice, bow once. Pray silently.
5. **Visit Kaitaku Shrine**: Those seeking romantic blessings should visit after the Main Sanctuary.

### Special Points of Interest

**Kaitaku Shrine**: A 3-minute walk from the Main Sanctuary. This shrine honors 37 pioneers who contributed to Hokkaido's development. Famous as a matchmaking power spot with popular "love fortunes" (¬•200).

**Third Torii Gate**: Also called the Park Entrance torii, this is the entrance from Maruyama Park side. Entering here offers about 10 minutes of forest bathing to the Main Sanctuary.

**Jingu Tea House**: A rest area within the grounds. The specialty "Hangan-sama" (grilled rice cake, ¬•100) is perfect after worship.

**Ezo Squirrels**: Many inhabit the grounds. You may spot these adorable creatures on the approach, especially in early morning.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much time should I allow for worship?**
A: About 30-40 minutes for the Main Sanctuary and Kaitaku Shrine. Including grounds exploration and tea house rest, allow 60-90 minutes.

**Q: Does the parking lot get crowded?**
A: Usually available, but extremely crowded during New Year's first three days, cherry blossom season (GW), and Sapporo Festival. Public transportation recommended.

**Q: What should I be careful of when visiting in winter?**
A: Watch for icy surfaces. Non-slip shoes or attachable spikes provide peace of mind. Also dress warmly.

**Q: Will I definitely see Ezo squirrels?**
A: As wild animals, sightings aren't guaranteed. Early morning offers the best chances. Note that feeding is prohibited.

**Q: Are pets allowed?**
A: Unfortunately, pets are not permitted in the shrine grounds.
