## The Allure of This Sacred Site

### History and Origins

Itsukushima Shrine's founding is attributed to Saeki no Kuramoto in 593 CE, the first year of Empress Suiko's reign. The principal deities are the three Munakata goddesses: Ichikishimahime-no-Mikoto, Tagorihime-no-Mikoto, and Tagitsuhime-no-Mikoto. As guardian deities of maritime traffic, they have gathered faith from those traveling the Seto Inland Sea since ancient times.

The shrine greatly expanded during the late Heian period under Taira no Kiyomori. As Governor of Aki Province, Kiyomori visited Miyajima and adopted Itsukushima Shrine as the guardian deity of the Taira clan. In 1168, he constructed the prototype of the present shrine buildings. During the era when "those who are not Taira are not human," Itsukushima Shrine commanded supreme authority and prosperity.

The shrine continued to receive warrior devotion after the Kamakura period. Mori Motonari, after his victory in the Battle of Itsukushima (1555), undertook repairs to the shrine buildings. During the Edo period, the shrine received patronage from the Asano family, lords of Hiroshima Domain, and continues to welcome numerous worshippers today. In 1996, it was registered as a World Cultural Heritage Site.

### Architecture and Natural Features

Itsukushima Shrine's most distinctive feature is its shrine complex built over the sea. The architecture incorporating Heian period shinden-zukuri style is designated a National Treasure. The Main Hall, Worship Hall, Purification Hall, Takabatai Stage, and Noh Stage are connected by corridors totaling approximately 275m in length‚Äîa grand composition.

The current Great Torii is the eighth generation, standing approximately 16.6m tall and weighing about 60 tons. Made from natural camphor wood, it is not buried in the seabed but stands by its own weight. At high tide, it appears to float in the sea; at low tide, visitors can walk right up to it.

Mount Misen (535m elevation) rising behind the shrine is known as a sacred mountain where Kukai trained. Its primeval forest is designated a Natural Monument. From the summit, visitors can enjoy panoramic views of the Seto Inland Sea's island-dotted beauty, easily accessible by ropeway.

### What Makes This Spot Special

Itsukushima Shrine is situated on what has been regarded as a "divine island," with the entire island considered a sacred body. For this reason, births and burials have traditionally been prohibited on the island, maintaining its sanctity. Even today, there is no cemetery on the island, and residents hold funerals on the opposite shore.

The shrine buildings, changing expression with the ebb and flow of tides, show various faces throughout a single day. The "shrine floating on the sea" at high tide, the "approach to the great torii" at low tide, and the "torii silhouetted against sunset"‚Äîdifferent mystical scenes await depending on when you visit.

Various Shinto rituals are held throughout the year. The "Kangen-sai" festival, held on a Sunday closest to the 17th day of the sixth lunar month, is a maritime festival said to have been started by Taira no Kiyomori. The fantastical sight of ceremonial boats crossing the sea accompanied by gagaku court music is unforgettable.

## Spiritual Benefits and Effects

### Known Spiritual Benefits

**Maritime and Traffic Safety**: The three Munakata goddesses are guardian deities of the sea. People have long come to pray for safe sea voyages. Today, this extends to general traffic safety.

**Business Prosperity and Financial Fortune**: As the shrine revered by Taira no Kiyomori, it is known for blessings of business prosperity and financial fortune. Entrepreneurs and business owners frequently visit.

**Victory Prayers**: Since Mori Motonari achieved victory in the Battle of Itsukushima, worshippers come seeking victory in competitions and contests.

**Matchmaking and Romance**: Ichikishimahime-no-Mikoto is also known as a goddess of beauty, drawing many women seeking good relationships.

**Artistic Improvement**: As a shrine where gagaku music and bugaku dance have been offered since the Heian period, it is also popular among those wishing to improve their artistic skills.

### The Unique Energy of This Site

Itsukushima Shrine is a place filled with energies of "purification" and "renewal." The sight of seawater flowing beneath the shrine buildings with the tides symbolizes washing away the old and welcoming the new.

Many worshippers report feeling their "heart cleansed" or "emotions reset" when standing before the great torii. It is known as a power spot that can encourage those visiting at life turning points or when wanting to make a fresh start.

## Visitor's Guide

### Basic Worship Etiquette

1. **Approaching the Shrine**: After disembarking the ferry, proceed along the main approach, bow to the great torii over the sea, then head to the shrine buildings.

2. **Purification at the Temizuya**: Take the ladle in your right hand to rinse your left, switch hands to rinse your right, switch again to pour water into your cupped left palm for rinsing your mouth, then rinse the ladle handle.

3. **Worship**: Two bows, two claps, one bow before the Main Hall. At Itsukushima Shrine, calming your heart quietly before worship is considered important.

## Area Information

### Nearby Attractions

**Mount Misen (30 min walk + ropeway)**
Sacred mountain at 535m elevation. Known as Kukai's training ground, the Reikado Hall at the summit contains the "Eternal Flame" that has burned for over 1,200 years. Ropeway round-trip ¬•1,840.

**Momijidani Park (10-min walk)**
Park planted with approximately 700 maple trees. Autumn foliage season (mid to late November) is spectacularly beautiful.

**Daisho-in Temple (15-min walk)**
Miyajima's oldest temple. Said to have been founded by Kukai, it houses many Buddhist statues.

**Toyokuni Shrine (Senjokaku) (5-min walk)**
Large sutra hall ordered built by Toyotomi Hideyoshi. Called "Senjokaku" (Hall of a Thousand Tatami) for its space equivalent to 857 tatami mats. Admission ¬•100.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I walk to the great torii?**
A: Only at low tide. Check tide tables before visiting. The difference between high and low tide is approximately 3-4m. About 2 hours after low tide is the best walking time.

**Q: How much time should I allow?**
A: About 1 hour for Itsukushima Shrine only; 3-4 hours including Mount Misen; a full day if leisurely exploring the entire island.

**Q: Does the goshuin line get long?**
A: Weekends, holidays, and autumn foliage season can mean 30-minute to 1-hour waits. Weekday or early morning visits recommended.

**Q: Is it enjoyable on rainy days?**
A: The corridors are roofed, so worship is possible in rain. Shrine buildings in misty rain have their own charm. However, Mount Misen trails become slippery, so take care.

**Q: What are the benefits of staying on Miyajima?**
A: Quiet morning worship, evening illumination (irregular), and strolling without day-trippers. The island has approximately 30 inns and hotels.
