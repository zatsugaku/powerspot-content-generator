## The Allure of This Sacred Site

### History and Origins

Matsushima's spiritual history dates back approximately 1,200 years to the Heian period. In 807, the legendary general Sakanoue no Tamuramaro visited during his military campaigns and was reportedly moved to tears by the scenery. In 828, the Buddhist monk Ennin (Jikaku Daishi) founded Enpukuji Temple, the predecessor to today's Zuiganji, establishing Matsushima as a center of faith.

During the Kamakura period, the regent Hojo Tokiyori praised Matsushima's beauty. In the Muromachi era, the poet Sogi declared it "the finest scenery in Japan." The golden age came during the Edo period when Date Masamune, the powerful lord of Sendai, undertook a massive reconstruction of Zuiganji Temple. He invested five years and employed 1.3 million workers to create the magnificent structures that stand today.

In 1643, the Confucian scholar Hayashi Shunsai officially designated Matsushima as one of Japan's Three Most Beautiful Views, alongside Amanohashidate and Miyajima.

### Architecture and Natural Features

Matsushima Bay contains approximately 260 islands, each covered with pine trees and vegetation, resembling natural bonsai floating on the water. The islands bear poetic names like Niojima (Deva King Island), Kaneshima (Bell Island), and Senganshima (Thousand Kan Island), each with its own legend.

Zuiganji Temple's main hall is designated a National Treasure and represents the pinnacle of Momoyama period architecture. The sliding door paintings decorated with gold leaf and vibrant colors reflect Date Masamune's aesthetic sensibilities and power.

Godaido Hall, the iconic symbol of Matsushima, was originally built in 807 by Sakanoue no Tamuramaro. The current structure, rebuilt by Date Masamune in 1604, is the oldest Momoyama-style building in the Tohoku region and is designated an Important Cultural Property.

### What Makes This Spot Special

The most distinctive feature of Matsushima is the natural "spiritual barrier" formed by its 260 islands. These islands encircle the bay, believed to block negative energies from the open sea while preserving peaceful, purifying energy within.

Remarkably, during the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake and tsunami, Matsushima's islands absorbed much of the tsunami's force, resulting in significantly less damage compared to neighboring coastal areas. Local residents speak of how "the islands protected us," reinforcing belief in Matsushima's mystical protective power.

The famous story of Matsuo Basho being rendered speechless by Matsushima's beauty during his "Narrow Road to the Deep North" journey speaks to the transcendent nature of this place‚Äîa beauty that defies description.

## Visitor's Guide

### Basic Etiquette

Matsushima's temples follow Buddhist practices:

1. **At the temple gate**: Bow once before entering; don't step on the threshold
2. **At the main hall**: Quietly press palms together in prayer
3. **Offerings**: Amount is not important; offer with sincerity
4. **When leaving**: Turn and bow once before departing

### Special Points of Interest

**Godaido's Sukashi-bashi Bridge**: The vermillion bridge leading to Godaido has gaps between the planks, allowing you to see the sea below. This design reminds worshippers to "approach with a pure and focused mind." Those uncomfortable with heights should proceed carefully.

**Rosary-Making at Entsuuin**: Create your own unique Buddhist rosary using natural stones of your choice. Sessions take about one hour and cost from ¬•1,000. These handmade rosaries serve as personal talismans.

**Ojima Island (Togetsukyo Bridge)**: Once a training ground for Buddhist monks, Ojima is considered the most spiritually powerful location in Matsushima. The island contains 108 rock caves historically used for memorial services. Explore with reverence and quietude.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much time should I allow for Matsushima?**
A: Allow 3-4 hours for main attractions (Godaido, Zuiganji, Entsuuin). Including a boat cruise and surrounding areas, plan for 5-7 hours.

**Q: Do I need to book sightseeing boats in advance?**
A: Reservations are generally not required. However, autumn weekends and holidays can be crowded; morning departures are recommended.

**Q: Does the parking lot get full?**
A: On weekends and holidays, especially during cherry blossom and autumn foliage seasons, parking can fill by 10 AM. Arrive early or use public transportation.

**Q: Can I enjoy Matsushima on rainy days?**
A: Yes! Zuiganji and Entsuuin have excellent indoor exhibits. Rain-shrouded Matsushima has its own atmospheric beauty.

**Q: Are pets allowed?**
A: Pets are not permitted on sightseeing boats or inside Zuiganji and Entsuuin. Godaido and the seaside promenade allow pets.
